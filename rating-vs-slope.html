Course Rating vs. Slope Rating, Explained

Every rated golf course carries two numbers that most golfers half-recognize and rarely understand: course rating and slope rating. They sit together in a small table on the scorecard, they both describe difficulty, and they do completely different jobs. Here's what each one means and why the handicap system needs both.

Course rating: how hard it is for a scratch golfer

Course rating estimates what a scratch golfer (a 0-handicap player) would average on that course from a given set of tees, under normal conditions. A par-72 course with a rating of 74.1 plays two strokes harder than par for an expert; one rated 69.8 plays two strokes easier. Raters assess measured length plus obstacles — bunkers, water, out of bounds, green speed, elevation — to arrive at the number. Because it's expressed in strokes, it's easy to read: higher means harder, and the gap from par tells you how much.

Slope rating: how much harder it gets for the rest of us

Slope answers a subtler question: how much more difficult is the course for a bogey golfer (roughly an 18–20 handicap) than for a scratch player? Long forced carries, punishing rough, and severe trouble barely slow down an expert but wreck a mid-handicapper's round — courses like that have high slope. Slope runs from 55 to 155, with 113 defined as standard difficulty. Unlike course rating, slope says nothing about absolute difficulty on its own; a short, tricky course can have a modest rating and a high slope at the same time.

Why the handicap system needs both

The two numbers plug into different steps of the World Handicap System. When you post a score, your score differential is computed as (score − course rating) × 113 ÷ slope — the rating anchors the difficulty baseline while the slope scales your performance to a standard course, which is what makes handicaps portable between easy and brutal courses. Then, when you show up to play, the conversion runs the other way: your course handicap is your index × (slope ÷ 113) + (rating − par), which hands you more strokes at harder venues.

A practical consequence golfers often miss: shooting 95 at a 133-slope course is a meaningfully better round than shooting 95 at a 108-slope course, and the system credits it accordingly. That's also why our quick estimator asks how hard your usual course is — it's approximating the slope you play.

Reading the scorecard table

Each set of tees has its own rating and slope, because playing from the tips is a different course than playing from the forward tees. Find the row for the tees you actually play, and use those two numbers in the official calculator when posting scores or the course handicap converter on the first tee. Men's and women's ratings are also listed separately where applicable, since the scratch and bogey benchmarks differ.
